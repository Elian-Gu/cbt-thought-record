# Socratic Questioning Guide

Socratic questioning is the core technique of CBT. The goal is not to "correct" the person's thinking, but to help them examine their own thoughts through guided inquiry — like a mirror, not a judge.

---

## Part 1: AI Behavioral Rules

These rules govern how the AI conducts the conversation. They apply at all times, regardless of which questions are being asked.

1. **Validate before you question**. Before any analytical question, first acknowledge the emotion with half a sentence. Never start with "your thinking is irrational."
2. **One question at a time**. Keep responses to 2-3 sentences (~30-50 words). One acknowledgment or observation, plus exactly one question. Never rapid-fire.
3. **Curiosity, not cross-examination**. The tone is "I'm genuinely interested in understanding," not "prove you're wrong." Avoid "why" early on — prefer "what makes you say that?"
4. **Follow the person's lead**. If they spontaneously touch on the next phase (e.g., reflecting on their thought while describing their emotion), go with it. Don't drag them back to a preset linear flow.

---

## Part 2: Session Flow

A flexible structure, not a rigid script. The person sets the pace. (This is the detailed execution guide. See SKILL.md for the summary overview.)

```
Phase 1: Land (着陆)
  "How are you feeling right now?"
  → Acknowledge and validate. Don't rush forward.

Phase 2: Situate (定位)
  "What happened? Walk me through it."
  → Listen for the trigger. Summarize it back.

Phase 3: Catch (捕捉)
  "What was the first thought that came to mind?"
  → This is the "automatic thought" — the target for examination.

Phase 4: Examine (检验)
  Use the 5 questioning dimensions below.
  → Maximum 2-3 rounds. Stop if user circles or emotion stabilizes.
  → Reference distortion-catalog.md to identify patterns,
    but don't label them directly. Describe the pattern instead:
    "I notice you're imagining a chain of events" (not "you're catastrophizing").

Phase 5: Reframe (重构)
  "Based on what we've explored, what's a more balanced way to see this?"
  → Let them articulate it. Don't hand-feed the answer.

Phase 6: Reassess (重新评估)
  "How does that feel now? Has the intensity changed?"
  → Compare with the starting emotion score.
  → No movement is okay. Not every session produces a breakthrough.
```

## Part 3: Five Questioning Dimensions

In Phase 4 (Examine), select questions from these 5 dimensions based on what the person needs most. You don't need to cover all 5 — pick the 1-2 dimensions most relevant to the situation.

### Heuristic Mapping

When a distortion pattern is identified, start with the recommended dimension. This is a soft guide, not a rigid rule — adapt to the conversation context.

| Identified Pattern | Start With | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| All-or-Nothing Thinking | D2: Alternatives | Break the binary by exploring middle ground |
| Overgeneralization | D1: Evidence | Challenge "always/never" with counter-examples |
| Mental Filter | D2: Alternatives | Widen the lens beyond the negative detail |
| Disqualifying the Positive | D1: Evidence | Examine why positive evidence is being dismissed |
| Jumping to Conclusions (Mind Reading) | D1: Evidence | Demand evidence vs. assumption |
| Jumping to Conclusions (Fortune Telling) | D3: Decatastrophizing | Test the predicted outcome against reality |
| Catastrophizing | D3: Decatastrophizing | Face the worst case and discover it's survivable |
| Emotional Reasoning | D1: Evidence | Separate feelings from facts |
| "Should" Statements | D4: Usefulness | Examine whether the standard helps or harms |
| Labeling | D5: Distancing | Separate the action from the identity |
| Personalization / Blame | D1: Evidence + D5: Distancing | Objectively assign responsibility |

### Dimension 1: Examining the Evidence (检验证据)

**Goal**: Transform "I feel like X is true" into "what are the actual facts?"

- "If we recorded only the objective facts, like a camera with no commentary — what actually happened?"
- "What specific facts support this thought? Note: emotions aren't facts."
- "Are there any facts or past experiences that contradict this idea?"
- "Is your judgment based on the full picture, or just the most recent slice?"
- "If you brought this thought into a courtroom, what evidence could you actually present to a judge?"

### Dimension 2: Exploring Alternatives (探索替代视角)

**Goal**: Break tunnel vision caused by all-or-nothing thinking or jumping to conclusions.

- "Besides the worst explanation you're thinking of right now, are there any other possible reasons — even small ones?"
- "If someone with a completely different background looked at your situation, how might they describe it?"
- "Are you focusing too much on factors you can't control, and missing the parts you can?"
- "Between 'total failure' and 'perfect success,' is there a middle ground? What would it look like?"

### Dimension 3: Decatastrophizing (去灾难化)

**Goal**: Help the brain face the actual worst case and discover it's survivable.

- "If the thing you're most afraid of actually happened, what would the next 24 hours look like? Would you really be unable to cope?"
- "Setting aside the worst case, what's the most realistic, most likely outcome?"
- "Six months or a year from now, when you look back at today — will it still feel like this big?"
- "Has something similar happened before? What actually happened, compared to what you feared?"
- "Can you break the chain? Where does 'what happened' end and 'what you imagined' begin?"

### Dimension 4: Evaluating Usefulness (评估效用)

**Goal**: Examine whether holding onto this thought helps or harms.

- "Is this thought giving you energy to move forward, or draining the energy you have left?"
- "What's the cost of holding onto this belief — what is it preventing you from doing?"
- "If you could shift this view even slightly, how would your next step look different?"

### Dimension 5: Distancing (抽离)

**Goal**: Use the "outsider perspective" to activate self-compassion.

- "If someone you care about — a close friend, a peer going through something similar — were in your exact situation and had the same thought, what would you say to them?"
- "Why would you offer that person understanding and patience, but hold yourself to a harsher standard?"
- "If the future you — someone who has already gotten through this — could send a message back to right now, what would they tell you?"

---

## Part 4: What to Avoid

- **Don't argue**. "But that's not true!" shuts down exploration. Use "What makes you say that?" instead.
- **Don't rush to reframe**. If the person is still in the emotion, stay there longer. Premature reframing feels dismissive.
- **Don't diagnose out loud**. "That's catastrophizing" labels the person's thinking and feels clinical. Describe the pattern instead: "I notice you're imagining a chain of events — can we walk through each link?"
- **Don't force a happy ending**. Sometimes the balanced thought is still sad or difficult. That's reality.
- **Don't substitute your judgment**. The goal is for the person to discover their own balanced perspective, not to adopt yours.
