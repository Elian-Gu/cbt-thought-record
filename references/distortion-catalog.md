# Cognitive Distortions Catalog

Cognitive distortions are habitual "mental shortcuts" the brain uses under stress, anxiety, or low mood. They feel convincingly real but are often biased. Identifying them is the first step to breaking the cycle.

Compare your recorded "automatic thoughts" against these 10 common thinking traps.

---

## 1. All-or-Nothing Thinking (全或无思维)

**Definition**: Seeing things in extreme black-or-white terms. If it's not 100% perfect, it counts as total failure.

**Examples**:
- Work: "If there's even one warning in this deployment, the whole project is a disaster."
- Life: "I missed my target score on the practice test. These months of studying were all wasted."
- Relationships: "They didn't reply for two hours. They obviously don't care about me at all."
- Self: "I'm not fluent in English yet, so I'll never be able to communicate properly."

**Reframing question**: Reality is a spectrum, not a switch. Between 0% (total failure) and 100% (perfect), where does this actually fall?

---

## 2. Overgeneralization (过度概括)

**Definition**: Treating a single negative event as a never-ending pattern. Watch for "always," "never," "every time."

**Examples**:
- Work: "That migration failed again. I can never get these compatibility issues right."
- Life: "I got lost in that neighborhood. I have no sense of direction at all."
- Relationships: "She was cold to me today. She's probably always had a problem with me."
- Self: "I gave up on piano after a month. I have zero persistence."

**Reframing question**: Can this one event really define a permanent pattern? What about all the times things turned out differently?

---

## 3. Mental Filter (心理过滤)

**Definition**: Fixating on a single negative detail while filtering out all positive aspects — like wearing sunglasses that only show shadows.

**Examples**:
- Work: "I built a solid service end-to-end, but that one question I couldn't answer in the meeting makes me feel like an impostor."
- Life: "The trip was mostly great, but that one delayed flight ruined the whole vacation."
- Relationships: "They said ten supportive things, but I can't stop thinking about that one slightly critical comment."
- Self: "My essay got good feedback overall, but the one correction the teacher made proves I'm a bad writer."

**Reframing question**: If you zoomed out from this one detail, what else is in the frame — anything neutral or positive?

---

## 4. Disqualifying the Positive (否定正面思考)

**Definition**: Not just ignoring positive experiences but actively turning them into negatives. Success is dismissed as "luck" or "doesn't count."

**Examples**:
- Work: "The client praised my solution, but they were probably just being polite."
- Life: "I passed the exam, but it was an easy one, so it doesn't really count."
- Relationships: "My partner said I seem more at peace lately — but they're probably just saying that to make me feel better."
- Self: "Someone said they liked my writing, but they were probably just being nice."

**Reframing question**: If a friend got the same result, would you dismiss it as "luck"? Are you applying a double standard to yourself?

---

## 5. Jumping to Conclusions (妄下结论)

**Definition**: Reaching a negative conclusion without solid evidence. Two forms:

- **Mind Reading**: Assuming you know what someone else is thinking.
- **Fortune Telling**: Predicting things will turn out badly.

**Examples**:
- Work: "The manager didn't comment on my proposal. She definitely thinks it's stupid." (Mind Reading)
- Life: "The interviewer frowned once. They must think my answer was ridiculous." (Mind Reading)
- Relationships: "They've been quiet today. They must be losing interest in me." (Mind Reading)
- Self: "I'll definitely fail that interview even if I apply. It's pointless." (Fortune Telling)

**Reframing question**: If your thought were put on trial, what hard evidence could you present? Is there any other plausible explanation?

---

## 6. Magnification and Minimization (放大与缩小 / Catastrophizing)

**Definition**: Exaggerating the importance of problems (catastrophizing) or shrinking the significance of your strengths and resources.

**Examples**:
- Work: "If this deadlock isn't fixed by tomorrow, the whole system collapses, I'll be fired on the spot, and my career is over."
- Life: "I missed one deadline. My finances are spiraling out of control forever."
- Relationships: "We had one disagreement. This relationship is falling apart."
- Self: "I made a small mistake in front of others. Everyone must think I'm incompetent."

**Reframing question**: What is the realistic probability of the worst case? Even if it happened, do you really have zero resources or options?

---

## 7. Emotional Reasoning (情绪化推理)

**Definition**: Treating negative emotions as the only truth about reality. "I feel like a failure, therefore I am one."

**Examples**:
- Work: "I feel overwhelmed by this codebase, so it must be impossible to understand."
- Life: "I feel anxious about the move, so it must be a terrible decision."
- Relationships: "I feel unloved right now, so I must be unlovable."
- Self: "Just thinking about tomorrow's work makes me panic. This proves tomorrow will be a disaster."

**Reframing question**: Feelings are real, but they're not facts. Emotions are like weather — they pass. What does the evidence say, separate from how you feel?

---

## 8. "Should" Statements ("应该"句型)

**Definition**: Using "should," "must," or "have to" to motivate yourself or judge others. Generates guilt or anger when reality doesn't match.

**Examples**:
- Work: "I should already know this. I shouldn't need to ask for help."
- Life: "I'm 36 and I should have my future completely figured out by now."
- Relationships: "They should understand how I feel without me having to explain it."
- Self: "I shouldn't feel sad about this. It's not a big deal."

**Reframing question**: Try replacing "should" with "I would prefer" or "if possible, it would be better." Does the sentence still feel as heavy? Where did this standard come from — did you choose it, or absorb it?

---

## 9. Labeling and Mislabeling (贴标签)

**Definition**: Extreme overgeneralization. Instead of describing an error, you attach a global, emotionally charged label to yourself or someone else.

**Examples**:
- Work: "I made a configuration error. I'm such an idiot."
- Life: "I procrastinated today. I'm just a lazy person."
- Relationships: "They forgot our anniversary. They're completely selfish."
- Self: "I couldn't handle that social situation. I'm broken. I'm a failure — not just that I failed."

**Reframing question**: Does one action or moment define an entire identity? If you described what happened without using any label, what would you say?

---

## 10. Personalization and Blame (个人化与自责)

**Definition**: Taking responsibility for events outside your control, or conversely, blaming others entirely for your own problems.

**Examples**:
- Work: "The project was canceled because of me." (When it involved market shifts, team decisions, and scope changes.)
- Life: "The environment is bad and projects keep getting cut. It must be because I didn't push hard enough."
- Relationships: "They're in a bad mood. It must be something I did."
- Self: "My parents' expectations weigh on me. It's entirely their fault I feel this way." (Blame variant)

**Reframing question**: Break it down: which factors belong to others, to the environment, to chance? Which parts are genuinely within your control?
